Part IV: The C.O.M.M.O.N.W.E.A.L.T.H. Vision & Philosophical Core

4.1 Vision Statement

The C.O.M.M.O.N.W.E.A.L.T.H. is a framework for a confederation of free municipalities. It is designed to foster a self-organizing, regenerative, and equitable socio-economic system by integrating a dynamic, voluntary economy with a robust, democratic, and ecological political culture. It aims to achieve this through institutionalized, face-to-face direct democracy, an ethical commitment to social ecology, and the empowerment of a civic-minded citizenry.

4.2 Core Principles

Communalism (Bookchin): The foundational political unit is the empowered, face-to-face democratic assembly of citizens. Freedom is realized through civic institutions, mutual responsibility, and direct democracy.

Social Ecology (Bookchin): All social and economic problems are rooted in ecological problems. A just society must be an ecological society, harmonizing human communities with the natural world through ethical and technological stewardship.

Counter-Economic Mutualism (Carson): The economy should be organized around decentralized, non-capitalist market dynamics, voluntary association, and countering all forms of privilege and monopoly, particularly those created or subsidized by the state.

Stigmergic Order (Wilbur): Complex, intelligent social coordination can emerge from simple agents responding to direct, local, and understandable signals ("digital pheromones") in their environment, fostering spontaneous, bottom-up organization.

Voluntary Economic Circulation (Gesell): The medium of exchange should be a public utility for transaction, not a tool for hoarding. Its circulation should be encouraged through voluntary, non-coercive economic incentives, ensuring capital serves the economy rather than dominating it.

Pragmatic Resilience (Gillis): The system must be designed to be anti-fragile, anticipating and gracefully handling its own potential failure modes, particularly those related to violence, coercion, and high-stakes conflict.

Part V: Civic Framework & Governance

5.1 The Civic Charter

The Commonwealth is governed by a formal Civic Charter, its constitution. The Charter outlines the core principles, laws, and the rights and responsibilities of all members. It can only be amended by a supermajority vote of the Civic Assembly.

5.2 Membership Tiers

Probationary: A new user who can transact but has no political rights or dividend access.

Verified: The standard economic status. Achieved via the Adaptive Trust model. Verified members are eligible for the Citizen's Dividend and can use the full economic suite of the system.

Citizen: The political status. Achieved when a Verified member formally and publicly ratifies the Civic Charter. Only Citizens may vote in the Civic Assembly, serve on committees, or join the Community Guard.

5.3 The Civic Assembly

The Assembly is the supreme policy-making body, composed of all Citizens.

It meets at regular, scheduled intervals for face-to-face or real-time digital deliberation.

It holds the sole authority to amend the Civic Charter, set overarching policies (including Ecological Covenants), authorize expenditures from the Public Goods Fund, and mandate the Community Guard.

5.4 The Technics Committee

A committee chartered by the Assembly, composed of randomly selected Citizens and voluntary experts.

Its role is to translate complex technical proposals into plain language and assess their adherence to the Civic Charter and Ecological Covenants before they can be put to a general vote by the Assembly, ensuring democratic oversight of technology.

Part VI: Economic Architecture

6.1 The Mutual Credit Network (MCN)

The core transactional layer is an interest-free, mutual credit system using the Community Work Credit (CWC) as its unit of account. The total CWC balance of the network is always zero.

6.2 Fund Collection & Hybrid Distribution

Collection:

Dynamic CWC Demurrage: A carrying cost is applied to positive CWC balances. The rate is not fixed; it is determined algorithmically by the supply and demand for credit on the P2P funding platform, rising when credit is scarce and falling when it is abundant.

Land Use Contribution (LUC): A fee representing the community-generated rental value of land, determined via the Use-Lease Auction mechanism.

Allocation:

All collected LUC flows into a General Community Fund.

By default, 100% of this fund is distributed directly and equally to all Verified members as a Citizen's Dividend.

Public Goods Pledge: Any Citizen may voluntarily pledge a percentage of their future dividends to the Public Goods Fund. This fund is used to finance community-approved projects (e.g., infrastructure, Commons Guilds) via a vote in the Civic Assembly.

6.3 Land & Tenure: The Dynamic Use Model

The Community Land Cooperative (CLC) registers Use-Leases based on the principle of possession through use.

Use-Lease Auctions: The system for determining the LUC is a market mechanism. Any member can bid what they believe the annual land rent of a parcel is worth. The current leaseholder must either match the bid (and begin paying that LUC to the community fund) or cede the lease to the bidder in exchange for direct payment for 100% of the market value of their improvements on the land. This ensures land migrates to its most highly-valued use and the community captures the full rental value.

6.4 Capital Formation

Reputation-Staked Credit: An individual's credit limit is not automatic. It is the sum total of CWC that other members have personally and knowingly "staked" against their reputation. If the debtor defaults, the stakers cover the loss, making credit a direct, P2P, risk-assessed relationship.

Decentralized Autonomous Ventures (DAVs): For-profit, equity-based enterprises. Founders can raise capital by issuing and selling "Venture Shares" to members, who then share in the profits and governance of the venture.

Federated Projects: A "DAV of DAVs." A structure for multiple DAVs to pool resources for massive, temporary industrial projects while retaining their individual sovereignty.

Commons Guilds: A formal, non-profit organizational structure for the social economy. Guilds (e.g., tool libraries, childcare co-ops) are member-governed and can be funded by the Public Goods Fund to manage common-pool resources and mutual aid.

Part VII: Identity, Trust, and Reputation

7.1 The Adaptive Trust Identity System

To prevent Sybil attacks, membership verification relies on a Web of Trust.

Dynamic Trust Threshold: To become "Verified," a new user must achieve a "total trust weight." Existing members can "attest" for the newcomer, with each attestation carrying a weight proportional to that member's own Attestation Score. The required threshold rises or falls based on overall network security conditions.

Merit-Based Path: A "Probationary" user who maintains a high reputation for a set period can be automatically elevated to "Verified" status, creating a path to membership that cannot be blocked by social cliques.

7.2 Multi-Faceted Reputation Ledger

The single "reputation score" is abolished. A user has several distinct, transparent, context-specific scores:

Transactional Score: Reliability in paying debts.

Attestation Score: Quality of users they have vouched for.

Community Score: Reliability in community-voted projects.

Domain-Specific Scores: Tagged scores for specific skills.

System functions now call on the relevant score (e.g., credit uses the Transactional Score).

Part VIII: Justice, Security, and Ecological Constraints

8.1 The Community Justice System

A tiered system for dispute resolution:

Tier 1 (Arbitration): For financial disputes, using CWC-based reputation bonds.

Tier 2 (Mediation): For interpersonal conflicts, using specialized, high-reputation mediators chosen by the parties.

Tier 3 (Assembly Mandate): For severe breaches of the Civic Charter.

8.2 The Community Guard

A voluntary, non-professional militia composed of Citizens, directly and exclusively accountable to the Civic Assembly.

The Guard can only be activated by a supermajority vote of the Assembly for specific, time-limited, defensive mandates as defined in the Civic Charter. It is the practical arm of the community's political will, not a private or for-profit entity.

8.3 The Ecological Constraint Layer

Ecological Covenants: The Civic Charter contains binding environmental standards set by the Assembly.

Impact Assessments: All new land use and major project proposals require a public assessment of their ecological impact.

Assembly Veto: The Assembly holds the ultimate authority to veto any project on ecological grounds.

Part IX: Inter-Commonwealth Relations

9.1 The Corsair Protocol

For external trade, the system uses a P2P atomic swap protocol. This allows individuals to trade CWC for external assets directly with each other via a temporary cryptographic escrow, avoiding the security and capital-flight risks of a centralized exchange.

Gateway Escrow Demurrage: To discourage using the platform for speculative hoarding of external assets, a user holding external crypto in their integrated wallet must lock a corresponding amount of CWC in an escrow contract that is subject to demurrage.

9.2 The Confederal Council

The ultimate political structure is confederal. Each sovereign Commonwealth may elect recallable delegates to a regional Confederal Council.

The Council has no independent authority; it is a forum for facilitating voluntary agreements on mutual defense, managing regional ecosystems, and coordinating shared infrastructure. All agreements must be ratified by the Civic Assembly of each participating Commonwealth to be binding.
