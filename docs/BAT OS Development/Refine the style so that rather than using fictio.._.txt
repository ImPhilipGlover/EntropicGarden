An Entry from the Guide on Economic Architecture

The Mutual Credit Network (MCN). The Mutual Credit Network is an elegant solution to a problem that, according to the anthropologist David Graeber, never actually existed in the way we think it did. The problem is the "myth of barter."

Economists, a type of people who are very good at explaining why their previous predictions were wrong, have long told a story about a time before money. In this time, people would wander around trying to swap things. A man who had a chicken but wanted a shoe would have to find a man who had a shoe but wanted a chicken. This is, when you think about it, a profoundly silly way to run a society, and would result in a lot of people having one shoe and a surplus of poultry.

The historical and anthropological record suggests that this almost never happened. For most of human history, local economies ran on credit. Or, to put it more simply, on favors. If you gave your neighbor a chicken, they owed you one. This was all recorded in the most advanced data storage device known to man: the human brain. These networks of "I owe yous" formed the basis of what we might call an economy.

The MCN is simply a digital version of this ancient system. It uses a Community Work Credit (CWC) as its unit of account. 21The total CWC balance of the network is always zero. 22 When a farmer gives a carpenter a bushel of apples for fixing his roof, the carpenter gets +10 CWC, and the farmer gets -10 CWC. No money changes hands. No interest is charged. It is a system built on the revolutionary idea that people can, and generally do, trust each other to return a favor.

This stands in stark contrast to systems built on coinage, which historically appears to have been invented primarily to pay soldiers to go and hit people from other societies over the head. The MCN, therefore, is a system for facilitating cooperation, not for funding armies. It is, in its own quiet way, an act of profound optimism. It is a good system.
