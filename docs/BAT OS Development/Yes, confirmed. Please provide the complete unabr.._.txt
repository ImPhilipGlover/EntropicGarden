6.3 Initial Specimen Entries:

6.3.1 Entry: Mutualism - A political and economic theory originating in the writings of Pierre-Joseph Proudhon116. It advocates for a society based on free association and voluntary contract, where individuals or collectives own their means of production and trade is conducted through an interest-free mutual credit system117. Its core tenet is "cost the limit of price."118.


6.3.2 Entry: Agorism - A revolutionary market anarchist philosophy developed by Samuel Edward Konkin III119. It advocates for the creation of a society free from coercion through "counter-economics"—the practice of engaging in the black market and gray market to starve the State of revenue and legitimacy120. The ultimate goal is a peaceful transition to a free market society121.


6.3.3 Entry: Demurrage - A carrying cost or tax on idle currency or capital122. In the context of the C.O.M.M.O.N.W.E.A.L.T.H. vision (4.2), it is a mechanism to incentivize circulation and investment, with the collected funds being redistributed as a Citizen's Dividend (4.3)123.


6.3.4 Entry: Decentralized Identifiers (DIDs) - A type of globally unique identifier that enables verifiable, decentralized digital identity. DIDs are controlled by the identity owner and do not depend on any centralized registry, identity provider, or certificate authority. They are a core component for building self-sovereign identity systems.

6.3.5 Entry: Verifiable Credentials (VCs) - A set of tamper-evident claims made by an issuer about a subject. In the C.O.M.M.O.N.W.E.A.L.T.H. context, a member could be issued a VC by a "Tool Guild" to prove they have the skill to operate a specific machine, which can then be presented to others in the network without revealing unnecessary personal data.

6.3.6 Entry: Gift Economy - A system of exchange where valuables are given without an explicit agreement for immediate or future rewards. These economies are often contrasted with market economies and focus on building relationships, mutual obligation, and community cohesion through the act of giving. This principle is partially embodied in the "Handshake Protocol".

6.3.7 Entry: Critiques of Mutualism - While foundational to the B.R.I.C.K. framework, Mutualism has been critiqued from other anarchist perspectives. Notably, some anarcho-communists question the use of any form of wages or markets, including the labor notes and mutual credit systems advocated by mutualists, arguing for the complete abolition of remuneration in favor of a system based on "from each according to his ability, to each according to his needs". Acknowledging this internal debate is crucial for systemic self-awareness.
